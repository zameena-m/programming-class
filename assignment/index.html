Jamming Since January: My top songs on loop in 2016

Jamming Since January

February 2016

To say this is a list of my favorite songs in 2016 is slightly misleading. Why? Because most of these the songs that I can't stop repeating were released years ago. Some of them are more current than others and the genres vary quite a bit. Check out what I've been vibing to in 2016.

Psychadelic rock

Puzzles

Unknown Mortal Orchestra

"The seven-minute song serves as a tacked-on coda with overdriven '70s hard rock guitar and Nielson stretching the capacity of his vocals to a bluesy peak. It will kill live, as will most of these songs which will bend into longer, louder psych forms in rock clubs. But "Puzzles" feels broad and out of place here, despite its charms." -- Jeremy D. Larson, Pitchfork

Indie folk

Broken Into Better Shape

Good Old War

"Consequently, Broken Into Better Shape proves true to its title, resulting in the band's most accessible effort yet and one that just might jettison them into prime time popularity." -- Lee Zimmerman, Pop Matters

Indie rock/jangle pop

Treat Her Better

Mac DeMarco

"It's only fitting that stick-with-the-girl songs “Let My Baby Stay” and “Treat Her Better” are almost as tunefully plush. In this context, DeMarco's non-album antics start to look like another defense mechanism, another way of cutting himself off at the legs before someone else does it for him." -- Mark Hogan, Pitchfork

Folk rock

Little Brave

Lucy Rose

"Rose's rise to prominence began as a vocalist with Bombay Bicycle Club, whose frontman Jack Steadman's influence was clearly a formative one on her soft and gently quavering voice."--Malcom Jack, The Guardian

Folk

Chateau Lobby #4 (in C for Two Virgins)

Father John Misty

"At various points in Father John Misty's musical account of his honeymoon, the eccentric folk-rocker and his newlywed celebrate "satanic Christmas Eve," "lift up your wedding dress someone was probably murdered in" and ruminate on how "dating for 20 years just feels pretty civilian." Playing out like one long, stream-of-consciousness verse, tinted with spaghetti-western horns, it's a self-centered love song that only Misty - and hopefully his wife - could relate to. But maybe that's the point." -- Kory Grow, Rolling Stone
